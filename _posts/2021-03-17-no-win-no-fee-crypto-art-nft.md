---
title: "No win, no fee! Breaking down NFTs"
---

All indicators suggest we're approaching peak bullshit. Over the past few months, I've found myself subjected to a lot of [unsubstantiated](https://verisart.medium.com/are-nfts-the-future-of-the-art-market-59bfe04cfed2) [claims](https://www.wired.co.uk/article/crypto-tokens) about how 'cryptoart' and [Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs)](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/03/11/arts/design/what-is-an-nft.html) will totally transform the artworld. There are many reasons—technological, legal, ethical, and [environmental](https://www.wired.com/story/nfts-hot-effect-earth-climate/)—for avoiding NFTs, but one claim is persistent, a claim that happens to shape the appeal of this technology for a lot of artists: "NFTs guarantee resale fees".

Caveat: I am not an economist, but I am a person who for the past decade has been involved with the artworld and has followed the development of blockchain cryptocurrencies, and I cannot see these claims bearing out. Rather than funding a new renaissance, NFTs will cross the event horizon of high-art & high-finance, where artworks and investments lose their classical distinction and become something approaching a singularity. Back in the real world, artists likely won't see a penny and will find themselves back at square one, where all the many reasons for avoiding the system still exist.

If you've heard of cryptoart and NFTs you've probably heard of [Beeple](https://www.beeple-crap.com/), aka Mike Winkelmann, and his digital project _Everydays: The First 5000 Days_. Beeple's work is profoundly ugly (and strangely few have commented on this fundamental fact), but Beeple's work sells. In fact, earlier this week, _Everydays_ sold at Christie's [for $69,346,250](https://onlineonly.christies.com/s/beeple-first-5000-days/beeple-b-1981-1/112924). The same amount could easily buy you a work by Pablo Picasso, Édouard Manet, Mark Rothko, or Vincent van Gogh, so what exactly is it that puts a collection of pixels on a par with the handmade work of a modern master?

Cryptoart is nothing more than art—images, text, music, videos—represented by NFTs, [unique digital certificates](https://ethereum.org/en/developers/docs/standards/tokens/erc-721/) embedded in the publicly accessible, permanent record of a blockchain. These certificates are made by using [cryptographic hashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_hash_function) to encrypt the metadata which represents the artwork—and just as it's difficult to break encryption, it's difficult to forge a certificate. The ownership of NFTs, and the ownership of the artworks they represent, can be transferred between accounts by making a payment on the blockchain and embedding the NFT in the transaction.

It's important to note that the technology behind NFTs isn't new: cryptographic hashes have existed for decades; blockchain technology was [designed in 2009](https://bitcoin.org/en/bitcoin-paper); the concept of 'art on the blockchain' has existed ever since then; and NFTs were [first outlined in 2017](https://github.com/ethereum/eips/issues/721). What is new is that you can find a growing number of online platforms offering to manage the creation of NFTs on your behalf. The promise these platforms make to artists is that they can use NFTs to authenticate their cryptoart, create limited editions of digital files, track their sales, and collect royalties and resale fees accordingly.

Resale fees? It may come as a surprise to many artists (and buyers & sellers) that many places around the world require resale fees to be paid to creators of artistic works. In the UK, resale fees are [set by law](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/artists-resale-right) at a maximum of 4% of the resale price, on any sale made within the copyright period (the artist's lifetime, plus 70 years). Because it's hard for artists to manage this themselves, companies exist to do the difficult work for them. In the UK we have The Design and Artists Copyright Society (DACS), which monitors gallery and auction sales, and organises payments for artists and their estates.

Despite this, it's not a perfect system, for the simple reason that galleries don't like paying resale fees, which cut into their slim profits (margins of 5% are not unheard of in so-called secondary sales). So, galleries go to lengths to hide their business and, fortunately for them, as there are few records of what is actually being sold, for how much, and to whom, they succeed. DACS, in turn, works on a spray & pray business model similar to TV Licensing in the UK or copyright trolls in the US; they spam companies with threats and demands and collect from the ones that buckle.

One of the few artists to successfully manage their secondary sales is the bête noire Banksy, whose high prices are maintained, in part, because he manages his own licensing company, [Pest Control](https://pestcontroloffice.com/faq.asp). Buyers want to know they're getting a genuine Banksy, so before a Banksy sale goes ahead, Pest Control authenticates the artwork and provides a (paper) certificate, while collecting the resale fees. If a gallery sells fakes or evades the fees, Pest Control will blacklist them—no certificate, no sale. Effectively, this central authority can control Banksy's prices by [regulating the supply of certificates](https://www.theartnewspaper.com/news/banksy-vies-for-control-of-resales-as-market-goes-ape). Banksy's model is _very_ successful. In fact, it bears _a lot_ of similarities to the promises made of NFTs. You might even call NFTs the Banksyfication of art—let a thousand Pest Controls bloom.

Proponents claim that NFTs will allow artists to control their market, gain an upper-hand on galleries, and ???? profit. So what are the problems with this model? I'm going to skip the technological, legal, ethical, environmental problems of tying your work to blockchains and trying to copyright every pixel in existence, as well as the fact that any artist has to make work that people actually want to own, and I'm going to say that the faith being placed in NFTs stems from a fairly naïve understanding of sales and value within the artworld, underestimating the degrees of abstractions by which artworks end up being represented.

It's strange, because NFTs are abstractions beyond compare. As Erin Griffith in the [New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/02/22/business/nft-nba-top-shot-crypto.html) puts it: "The buyers are usually not acquiring copyrights, trademarks or even the sole ownership of whatever it is they purchase. They're buying bragging rights and the knowledge that their copy is the 'authentic' one." It's evident that there's no value in the artworks themselves, it's found in something else, something intangible, which seems at odds with the principle of _Non-Fungible Tokens_. The claim that NFTs "can't be divided, edited, or destroyed" is correct in the strict sense—you can't cut a certificate in half and claim each half is valid—but, you _can_ issue new certificates, and this is where things get complicated…

Artists expect that once I buy an NFT artwork, at some point I'll resell the NFT and they'll see a resale fee—say 10%. But, given anyone can make NFTs, what's there to stop me creating my own NFT, which certifies ownership of the original NFT, and selling mine instead? In this case, any resale fees would go to me. I could even undercut the original NFT's resale fee, from 10% to 2%, to attract buyers. If any of the subsequent buyers of my new NFT wanted to transfer the original NFT to their own account, I would simply 'sell' them the original for an insignificant amount, say $0.01. Even if it were the tenth resale of the artwork, only two payments would have been made to the artist: my original purchase, and the fee for the $0.01 sale.

…and why stop here? In this decentralised system there's nothing to stop me producing 100 NFTs, each guaranteeing 1% of the income of any future sales of an artwork, and releasing them to the market. Or, why not bundle up a set of worthless NFTs and trade them together, or even sell shares of the bundle. In fact, NFTs are [designed to work in this way](https://billyrennekamp.medium.com/re-fungible-token-rft-297003592769), they are _extensible_. I'm not surprised that these complex technical and financial facts appear to have been overlooked by artists, but I can't see how they can be ignored.

None of this is hypothetical, this is how artworks have been traded _for centuries_. I'm sure that the same conversation played out in 17ᵗʰ-century Amsterdam, when the commercial art market began to take form alongside the invention of joint-stock companies and other financial instruments—"It's impossible to own half a Rembrandt, and anyway, we'll always know whose house it's in." Today, artworks are bought and sold by syndicates protected by shell companies, and investors are rarely so gauche as to actually have valuable artworks hung in their own home. Why bother with the hassle of insurance, tax, and customs inspections when you can simply host a champagne reception in the viewing booth [at your freeport](https://www.artsy.net/article/artsy-editorial-freeports-operate-margins-global-art-market)?

However, all the attention paid to major sales overlooks the fact that art is a [bad investment](https://www.ft.com/content/6fc3b7da-a8e4-11e7-ab55-27219df83c97). Auction houses make sure that you always hear the headline-grabbing figures, but never about the canvases which are worth less than their wooden frames. One reason that art is so risky is because people tend to want to keep their artworks—and their bragging rights—and sell them only at the last opportunity, long after their value has peaked. But the fundamental barrier to investment in art is the lack of information. This is deliberate: sellers don't want people to know how much money they are making (or losing). This makes analysis difficult, which in turn makes investment no better than a leap of faith. If NFTs will be used for anything, they'll be used in an attempt to break this secrecy and create market indices for analysis.

Note: I am not saying that no-one will make money from NFTs, what I am saying is that while you may get suckers and gloaters whose belief in [conspicuous consumption](https://circaartmagazine.net/a-celestial-cyberdimension-art-tokens-and-the-artwork-as-derivative/) makes them pay outright, and certainly you'll get people involved in the industry [pumping up auction prices](https://nettime.org/Lists-Archives/nettime-l-2103/msg00041.html) with their venture capital—_stop press_: the buyer of Beeple's artwork was revealed as none other than the [founder of Metapurse](https://amycastor.com/2021/03/14/metakovan-the-mystery-beeple-art-buyer-and-his-nft-defi-scheme/), the largest NFT fund in the world—the majority of large sales will be made using complex financial and legal instruments. This is because people who do buy art as an investment tend to treat them _as investments_. Artworks are kept locked up, hidden by Swiss mountains and Cayman Island companies, represented only by mysterious figures in accounts. They will use NFTs to facilitate investment that is so far removed from the actual artworks that most artists won't notice a thing.

I admit that this degree of financialisation is unlikely for lower-value works, however these works have a smaller secondary market. And once their makers have factored in the transfer fees, exchange fees, and price volatility associated with cryptocurrencies, as well as all the risks of using badly-coded, internet-connected, p4ssw0rd-protected wallets, they may begin to wonder why they even bothered.

It's right to call this [a mania](https://www.nytimes.com/2021/03/11/arts/design/nft-auction-christies-beeple.html), few people are thinking critically and I can understand why. The isolation measures put in place to combat the coronavirus pandemic have led to boredom, job losses, and an interest in get-rich-quick schemes. This, combined with the recent spike in bitcoin prices and the GameStop saga, has pushed new financial technologies to the forefront of public awareness. So it was inevitable that precariously employed and undervalued producers of all sorts of media would eventually stumble upon a technology that promises instant, guaranteed returns. _No win, no fee!_

A brief survey of social media finds 'multi-level-marketing' pyramid schemes aimed at enticing young women into selling cosmetics, and money laundering scams promoted by deranged, Teflon-suited rise-n-grind LinkedIn recruiters—people too young remember Beanie Babies™, Pokémon cards, or the Global Financial Crisis—and others predicting a [future economy of neo-feudal patronage](https://officehours.substack.com/p/165-nfts-and-the-dna-rule). Like Renaissance masters, creators will sell high-value artwork to rich patrons and live off the accumulated resale fees, using their ample free time to create the work that they actually enjoy. Personal experience tells me the opposite is more likely; artists will continue scrabbling around for the diamond in the dungheap.

The unconvinced may see NFTs like [Pascal's Wager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_wager)—given a potential finite loss versus infinite gain, why not enter into it if it'll pay off in the long-term, or even just once? Well, now we're back to discussing the [long-term viability](https://flash---art.com/2021/02/episode-v-towards-a-new-ecology-of-crypto-art/) of this system, and if you're thinking about that, you might want to think about whether your [datacentre will be underwater](https://everestpipkin.medium.com/but-the-environmental-issues-with-cryptoart-1128ef72e6a3).
